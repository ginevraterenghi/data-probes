Drawing on participatory design and data physicalization, the research examines how knowledge emerges through material engagement, interaction, interpretation, and collective sense making. Abstract

This doctoral research investigats how data physicalization can support awareness and communication of symptoms in women affected by urinary tract infections (UTIs). Through a design approach, the study explores how personal and bodily experiences can become shared and collectively valued forms of knowledge. Drawing from feminist theories of situated knowledge and biosemiotics, symptoms are reconsidered as interpretative processes that acquire meaning through the continuous interaction between body, perception, and environment, rather than as fixed biomedical evidence. Within this frame, the research advances the concept of physical variables, which are material properties such as texture, weight, and shape, conceived as a participatory device for knowledge generation.

UTIs are among the most common bacterial infections, disproportionately affecting women and often recurring over time. Communication around symptoms, however, remains limited because clinical practice privileges measurable biological indicators, while lived experience is often marginalised. In response to this gap, and drawing on autographic design, the research adopts a participatory approach in which material engagement enables embodied sensations to take shape. Through participatory data physicalization, making becomes a process of knowing, as participants work with tangible materials to express, reflect on, and communicate their bodily experiences. This process supports awareness of symptoms while creating shared forms through which experiences of disease can be articulated and discussed.

Five workshops were conducted between 2023 and 2024 in Switzerland and the United Kingdom, involving around fifty participants. Three sessions, involving women affected by recurrent UTIs and clinicians, form the analytical core of the research. Participants used a set of modular components defined as Data-Probes, guided by symptom and physical-variable labels and a structured workshop protocol, to construct artefacts that represent their interpretation of symptoms. The findings show that engagement with physical variables enabled participants to articulate sensations and emotions often excluded from biomedical accounts. By translating experiences such as pressure, pain, or burning into tangible artefacts, participants were encouraged to reflect on and compare their experiences, recognise patterns, and share interpretations. Through this process, material engagement transformed individual sensations into collective insights by providing shared reference points that made experiences visible and comparable. This supported dialogue and awareness of embodied conditions, while also creating a common structure for interpreting and analysing symptoms collectively.

In this direction, the main methodological contribution is the development of the Physical Variable Apparatus, a material-discursive arrangement that translates theoretical foundations into participatory practice by connecting labels, protocols, material components, and a digital interface into a system that supports participation, physical engagement, and interpretation. Beyond UTIs, the approach offers a transferable model for embodied data into participatory enquiry. Future work could extend the Apparatus to other chronic or subjective conditions and integrate digital or computational tools to connect embodied knowledge with medical data systems. The study contributes to design theory and practice by showing how data physicalization can act as a generative process for collective knowledge production, making embodied experience visible, interpretable, and meaningful across contexts.


Contribution to knowledge

Situated at the intersection of design research, data physicalization, and women's healthcare, in this thesis, I propose new ways of engaging with embodied experience as a valid and communicable form of knowledge. Through the development and application of the Physical-Variable Apparatus, the research advances methodological, theoretical, and empirical understanding of how material engagement can support participatory knowledge creation in healthcare contexts.

Methodologically, I introduce the physical variables as a means to elicit, structure, and interpret embodied data. Applied through participatory data physicalization workshops, these variables guide the translation of perceptions into tangible and discussable forms, enabling participants to express and compare experiences collectively. The development of Data-Probes, as material components, formalises this process, turning acts of making and reflection into systematic methods for capturing and analysing embodied knowledge. Complementing this, a cross-format digital interface integrates results from material artefacts, narratives, and symptom–variable associations, supporting multimodal analysis and interpretation. Together, these contributions reposition data physicalization as a methodological approach for observing and analysing the processes through which knowledge emerges rather than representing predefined data.

Theoretically, I support data physicalization as a generative and epistemic process. By introducing physical variables as instruments that actively shape knowledge production, I want to demonstrate how materiality participates in meaning-making rather than merely representing information. This perspective reframes data as interpretive and relational, co-constructed through the interaction between people, materials, and perception. The work thus extends the field toward a process-oriented understanding of how design can contribute to situated and participatory forms of knowing.

Empirically, the thesis contributes to understanding women's experiences of urinary tract infections by articulating how participatory and material practices can enrich clinical perspectives. The findings reveal the importance of psycho-emotional and relational factors in symptom experience and communication. Through the use of material and reflective practices, participants generated new descriptors and metaphors that expanded the vocabulary of illness, making embodied experiences visible and comparable. These articulations fostered dialogue between patients and clinicians and among individuals with similar conditions, highlighting how participatory design can complement medical data with patient-led forms of understanding.

Overall, the thesis establishes a design-led approach to healthcare knowledge that recognises embodied data as co-produced through participation. By integrating participatory design, material engagement, and interpretive analysis, it contributes to more inclusive and situated forms of communication and awareness around the body and illness experience.


1. INTRODUCTION

In this doctoral research, I examine how design can contribute to the understanding and communication of embodied experiences in the healthcare scenario. Through a design method, I explore how material, participatory, and data informed approaches support the articulation of these experiences and enable forms of knowledge and dialogue within clinical contexts. This PhD research originated within SPEARHEAD (SwissPandemic AMR Health Economy Awareness Detect), a four-year project (2022–2025) funded by the Swiss Innovation Agency (Innosuisse). SPEARHEAD brings together a consortium of eight public and four private Swiss institutions with the aim of developing multidisciplinary responses to antimicrobial resistance (AMR). The project adopts a cross sectoral perspective that combines scientific, technological, and social expertise to promote responsible antibiotic use through prevention, stewardship, and public engagement (SPEARHEAD, n.d.). Within this consortium my role was as a collaborator at the Institute of Design (IDe) of the University of Applied Sciences and Arts of Southern Switzerland (SUPSI), where Dr Serena Cangiano led the Subproject 3, that seeks to involve citizens and raise public awareness of AMR.

MAKEAWARE!, which is the name of the initiative developed for this purpose, employs design approaches to make complex health issues tangible and understandable, developing interactive and data-based activities that support reflection, dialogue, and participation (Cangiano et al., 2024). My contribution focused on the development of the engagement strategy and the design of participatory activities. This work provided the initial context for the doctoral research and introduced the interdisciplinary environment from which the research topic progressively evolved. It highlighted the crucial role patients play as holders of experiential knowledge about their condition, knowledge that is not always acknowledged or integrated into diagnostic practices. This gap underscored the importance of recognising how personal experience and clinical procedures intersect in everyday care. Guided by these insights, the research began to focus on urinary tract infections (UTIs) as an embodied experience, one that can be most accurately described and interpreted by those who live with the condition. This shift drew attention to the communicative challenges faced by patients and the ways in which interactions with healthcare providers influence treatment decisions. This process shaped the direction of the thesis, which is outlined in the following sections and visually summarised in Figure 1.1.


1.1 Research problem

Difficulties in describing and interpreting symptoms form the starting point for this research, particularly when these descriptions are expected to contribute meaningfully to diagnosis. This challenge becomes especially evident in the context of urinary tract infections (UTIs), which account for a substantial proportion of antibiotic use. Their high frequency, tendency to recur, and frequent reliance on empirical prescribing mean that even small uncertainties in how symptoms are expressed or understood can influence patterns of antimicrobial consumption on a wider scale. For many women, UTI symptoms are intimate, changeable, and sometimes ambiguous, and their description is not always afforded sufficient trust during clinical encounters. When patients feel uncertain about how to express their experiences, or when their accounts are not recognised as meaningful forms of evidence, a communication gap emerges between patients and clinicians.

This gap shapes treatment decisions and can limit opportunities for shared understanding and participation in care. In this wider landscape, improving the perception and communication of symptoms is essential for strengthening treatment pathways and reducing avoidable antibiotic use. Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) represents a growing global health concern. Although resistance is a natural biological process, its progression is accelerated by extensive antibiotic use across healthcare, agriculture, and everyday life. Without meaningful change, resistant infections are projected to cause up to ten million deaths annually by 2050 (O’Neill, 2016). Enhancing how symptoms are understood, articulated, and trusted in clinical practice is therefore an important part of responding to AMR. Supporting clearer and more confident symptom communication can lead to more appropriate treatment decisions and contribute to broader efforts to limit unnecessary antibiotic consumption.

Based on this issue, I formulated the following research questions to guide the theoretical and methodological trajectory of this thesis. They have been shaped and organised in a progressive logic. The first examines how participatory design enables women to contribute actively to the creation and interpretation of their data. The second explores how material engagement transforms personal experiences into tangible and communicable forms. The third introduces physical variables as the set of relations through which these expressions are organised, compared, and shared to support collective understanding. I present each research question with its specific objective and methodological orientation. Because the study adopts an exploratory approach, the rationale for each question is included to clarify the intentions that guide their development. RQ1. How can participatory design practices expand healthcare enquiry by enabling women to articulate embodied experience and contribute to the co-creation of knowledge? Objective: The first question positions design within healthcare and investigates how participatory practices can create new forms of engagement between women affected by UTIs, clinicians, and researchers. The objective is to explore how women can take an active role in describing their symptoms and contribute to more contextual and meaningful data that can complement biomedical information.
Approach: This question is addressed through a review of literature across design, feminist theory, and healthcare studies. In parallel, semi-structured interviews with medical experts working on antimicrobial resistance help to clarify the role of data within the patient scenario and to identify opportunities for improving awareness and communication, including the types of information that are most useful for diagnosis. The insights gained from this combined enquiry inform the design of the Shape Your Symptoms workshop format, where participants collectively explore and describe their experiences through co-creation and reflection.
Rationale: Healthcare systems have traditionally privileged standardised knowledge, often marginalising lived and gendered experiences. Participatory design provides a way to redress these imbalances by involving women as co-investigators in the process of understanding illness. Through collaborative and reflective activities, design practices make it possible to surface embodied experiences that are difficult to communicate, thereby strengthening awareness, agency, and understanding. RQ2. How can material engagement support the interpretation and communication of intimate and embodied experiences within participatory data physicalization practices? Objective: The second question focuses on the role of materiality in making bodily sensations perceptible and communicable. This question investigates how tactile and visual exploration enables participants to translate personal experiences into shared and interpretable forms.

Approach: To address this question, the study introduces Data-Probes, which are material components with different textures, weights, and qualities. Through hands-on interaction with these components during participatory workshops, women affected by UTIs construct artefacts that embody their sensations. These artefacts are documented, analysed, and discussed to understand how material engagement supports reflection and communication.
Rationale: Material engagement transforms symptom description into a reflective and collective process. Through direct interaction with materials, participants externalise personal sensations and develop new approaches to perceive and communicate their experiences. The process supports introspection and dialogue, allowing sensitive or stigmatised aspects of health to be discussed through external and tangible elements. In this way, materiality becomes an active agent in the creation of meaning and the sharing of embodied knowledge. RQ3. How can physical variables transform individual interpretations of embodied experience into collective knowledge? Objective: The third question introduces physical variables as the main contribution through which embodied data are structured and interpreted. Variables are conceived to formalise how material properties such as texture, weight, and shape can support the transition from individual experiences into expressions that can be compared, discussed, and understood collectively.
Approach: The research analyses the artefacts and narratives produced during the workshops to identify recurring links between symptoms and material properties. These links form the basis for defining physical variables as shared structure that connect individual experiences. This process leads to the development of the Physical-Variable Apparatus, which organises the relationship between participation, material engagement, and interpretation.
Rationale: Physical variables provide a conceptual and methodological connection between personal experiences and shared understanding. Variables enable participants to reflect upon their expressions and align their subjective sensations at can be described in comparable ways while preserving their specificity. In this way, the variables position data physicalization not as a technique for representation but as a process through which knowledge is generated collaboratively.
In this thesis, collective knowledge refers to the shared understanding that emerges when women affected by UTIs express, interpret, and compare their embodied experiences through participatory and material practices. Through co-creation, discussion, and the use of material artefacts, individual sensations become articulated in ways that allow participants and clinicians to recognise patterns, differences, and relations across experiences. 1.3 Research aims

The aim of this doctoral research is to advance understanding of how embodied experiences of UTIs can be communicated and recognised within clinical consultations and broader diagnostic processes. The study responds to the persistent gap between women’s lived experiences and the standard forms of information that guide diagnosis and treatment. This gap reflects broader limitations in how healthcare systems and clinical practices engage with subjective, intimate, and context-dependent forms of knowledge. In this direction, my intention is to develop and study design-based approaches that support women in expressing embodied sensations in ways that are perceptible, interpretable, and meaningful for clinical decision making and patient–clinician communication. By doing so, the study contributes to more responsive and inclusive forms of symptom communication.

More specifically, the research aims to bring lived experience into closer dialogue with clinical knowledge; support women in developing awareness of their bodily sensations and expressing them through participatory and material practices; explore how material engagement can help transform subjective sensations into communicable expressions; improve communication between patients and healthcare providers by introducing expressions of symptoms that complement biomedical accounts; strengthen data physicalization as an approach for generating knowledge that connects individual experiences with shared understanding in healthcare contexts. These aims establish the conceptual purpose of the research and frame the methodological direction that follows.


1.4 Research objectives

To pursue these aims, I structured the research around the following objectives. Each objective describes a concrete step that enables the study to meet its broader purpose. The objectives identified are the following:
1) Develop participatory practices for expressing embodied experience: Design and conduct workshops that enable women affected by UTIs to articulate symptoms, exchange experiences, and reflect on the meanings they assign to bodily sensations.
2) Investigate material engagement as a means of interpretation: Introduce material components that support participants in externalising embodied sensations and translating them into tangible and communicable forms.
3) Establish physical variables as structures for shared understanding: Analyse the artefacts and narratives produced during the workshops to identify material properties such as texture, shape, weight or density that can organise individual expressions into patterns that aid comparison and interpretation.
4) Produce a methodological contribution for healthcare communication: Synthesise insights from participatory and material practices to form a coherent method that can support patient agency, enrich contextual knowledge, and engage clinicians with embodied experience through a design methodology. These objectives translate the aims into specific actions that shape the design and execution of the research.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL BACKGROUND TO CO-CREATE KNOWLEDGE FROM SYMPTOMS

In this chapter, I introduce urinary tract infections (UTIs), addressing this disease as both a clinical issue and a gendered health concern. UTIs are among the most common bacterial infections, causing significant personal discomfort and contributing to the global problem of antimicrobial resistance (AMR). Diagnosis often relies on patients’ descriptions of their symptoms, which, while essential, are inherently subjective. Misinterpretation of these accounts can lead to misdiagnosis, recurrent infections, and unnecessary antibiotic use. Even more specifically, the human consumption of antibiotics is the main driver of resistance development and, for this reason, limiting antibiotic use has been highlighted as a prime policy concern in efforts to address antimicrobial resistance (Adda, 2020). Although UTIs can affect individuals of all genders, women experience them disproportionately due to anatomical, hormonal, and behavioural factors. This disparity extends beyond biology, revealing how gender influences susceptibility, symptom communication, and treatment within a healthcare system that has historically underrepresented women in research and undervalued their experiences (Almeida et al., 2016; Perez, 2019). Addressing UTIs, therefore, requires confronting not only biological factors but also structural biases that shape how symptoms are perceived, recorded, and acted upon. The subjective and embodied nature of symptoms demands methods that move beyond detached observation and externally imposed data collection. Conventional biomedical practices often extract patient accounts as impersonal data to be recognised through a process of standardisation by professionals, stripping them of context and diminishing their relevance to those experiencing illness. In contrast, this research adopts the concept of situated knowledge (Haraway, 1988), emphasising that meaningful representations of health must be co-created with those who live with the condition.

Within this context, symptoms are framed as material traces that acquire meaning through interpretation. Symptoms are understood as manifestations of internal states that become significant through bodily perception and interaction. This framing builds on two key perspectives: biosemiotics considers symptoms as bodily signs within an ongoing process of signalling and meaning-making, while autographic design extends this view by treating these signs as self-inscribing qualities that can be revealed and interpreted through material engagement and data physicalization. Together, these perspectives recognise the body as both the source and interpreter of its own evidence. To enable this interpretation to emerge and to support understanding between individuals with lived experience of UTIs and medical professionals, participation becomes a means of connecting scientific knowledge with everyday realities. Through participatory engagement, mutual learning is fostered and insights are produced that no single actor could generate alone (van Wart et al., 2020). Participation is not framed as an additional step but as an integral practice through which participants actively shape how phenomena are explored, understood, and communicated. Grounded in design practices, participatory processes support people in collecting, interpreting, and communicating their own data, transforming situated personal interpretations into knowledge that holds value at a collective level. In this chapter, I present the theoretical background that supports the study and outline the literature on which it is based. The literature review and the theoretical background are introduced as a single, integrated process rather than as separate stages. Insights from design, healthcare, feminist theory, and data practices, organised and shown in Figure 2.1, come together to guide the methodological direction developed in Chapter 3.


2.1 The problem of UTIs within women’s health

UTIs are faced within this work as both a clinical challenge and a gendered concern. UTIs are among the most widespread bacterial infections, causing significant personal suffering and contributing to the growing problem of antibiotic consumption and resistance. Diagnosis often relies on patients’ accounts of their symptoms, which, while essential, are inherently subjective. Misinterpretation of these accounts can lead to misdiagnosis, recurrence, and unnecessary antibiotic use. As women are disproportionately affected, and their experiences are often undervalued due to structural gender bias in medical data and practice, feminist perspectives are introduced to challenge these exclusions, recognising women’s embodied knowledge as crucial for advancing more equitable and effective care. 2.1.1 UTI disease concerns UTIs are among the most frequent bacterial infections worldwide, with an estimated 150 million cases each year (Mohiuddin, 2019). They represent a major public health concern due to their high incidence, impact on quality of life, and significant contribution to antibiotic consumption. Accurate diagnosis of UTIs is essential to ensure effective treatment and to avoid unnecessary use of antibiotics. However, diagnosis often depends heavily on how patients describe their symptoms during medical consultations. This reliance on patient narratives presents significant challenges. Symptoms such as pain, burning, urgency, or discomfort are personal experiences. They are variably expressed, influenced by personal perception, cultural factors, and communication styles, and they are sometimes incomplete or inconsistent (Masajtis-Zagajewska & Nowicki, 2017). However, they are fundamental in guiding critical treatment decisions, often before laboratory confirmation is available. The tension between objective signs and subjective symptoms defines the central difficulty of UTI diagnosis. Objective indicators, such as urine culture results or the presence of fever, are prioritized in medical practice because they are measurable and verifiable. However, these results are not always accessible when decisions need to be made. Laboratory tests require time, and in many primary care settings, they are unavailable at the moment of consultation (Ribers and Ullrich, 2022). As a result, physicians must often make decisions based on incomplete information, relying on the patient’s description of their condition. Women’s involvement and causes of infection Women experience UTIs far more frequently than men, with about 81% of all UTIs reported in women (Kaur and Kaur, 2020). An estimated 40 to 50 percent of women will have at least one episode during their lifetime, compared with around 5 percent of men (Mohiuddin, 2019). This disparity stems primarily from anatomical and physiological differences. The female urethra is shorter, meaning bacteria have less distance to travel before reaching the bladder and less time to be removed by urination. The urethral opening is also positioned closer to the anus, allowing easier transfer of uropathogenic bacteria from the perineal area to the bladder. Additionally, the vaginal and periurethral environments are more susceptible to microbial colonization, particularly during periods of hormonal change (Pigrau and Escolà-Vergé, 2020). The presence of the vaginal cavity and the close proximity of the rectum to the urethral opening create favorable conditions for bacterial colonization. Beyond these anatomical factors, several other risk factors contribute to recurring UTIs in women, including behavioral factors, genetic susceptibility, age-specific factors, pregnancy-related factors, and urinary catheterization. Several biological and behavioural conditions further increase susceptibility. Menopause reduces levels of estrogen, which can alter the vaginal flora and diminish natural protection against pathogens. Diabetes compromises immune function and increases glucose in urine, providing a favourable environment for bacterial growth. Behavioural factors include frequent sexual activity, sexual initiation at an early age, recent changes in sexual partners, and the use of spermicides, all of which disrupt normal flora and facilitate bacterial colonisation. Previous antibiotic use is also a contributing factor, as it can disturb the protective balance of lactobacilli in the vaginal microbiota, leaving women more vulnerable to infection (Pigrau and Escolà-Vergé, 2020). Across these cases, the primary pathogen responsible for UTIs is Escherichia coli, which causes more than 80 percent of infections. Other microorganisms, such as Staphylococcus saprophyticus, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Enterobacter spp., Proteus mirabilis, and Enterococcus faecalis, are also involved, particularly in recurrent or complicated cases (McLellan and Hunstad, 2016). The clinical presentation of UTIs depends on the site of infection. Lower urinary tract infections, commonly referred to as cystitis, typically cause symptoms such as dysuria, urinary frequency, and discomfort. Upper urinary tract infections, including pyelonephritis, present more severe manifestations such as fever, chills, nausea, and flank pain, reflecting a more systemic inflammatory response (Masajtis- Zagajewska and Nowicki, 2017). Because laboratory confirmation requires time and is often unavailable at the point of care, treatment is frequently initiated during the first consultation based on reported symptoms and patient history (Ribers and Ullrich, 2022). This reliance creates a diagnostic risk, as when symptoms are vague, unclear, or misinterpreted, the treatment may not target the actual cause of infection. A patient may receive an antibiotic that is ineffective against the bacteria responsible, which can reduce the likelihood of full recovery and prolong illness (Masajtis-Zagajewska and Nowicki, 2017). To minimize this risk under uncertainty, clinicians frequently prescribe broad-spectrum antibiotics designed to treat a wide range of bacteria (OECD, 2019). While this strategy can offer immediate relief, it has unintended consequences, as broad-spectrum drugs disrupt the balance of normal bacteria in the body, potentially causing side effects and leaving behind resistant strains that are harder to treat (Holmes, 2016). Recurrence and chronic burden Inadequate or inappropriate therapy may temporarily relieve symptoms without fully eradicating the infection, increasing the likelihood of recurrence. Between 20 and 30 percent of women experience a recurrent infection following an initial episode (McLellan and Hunstad, 2016). Recurrent UTIs are defined as two or more episodes within six months or three or more within twelve months. This leads to considering the impact extending beyond physical symptoms, as repeated pain, discomfort, and disruption to daily life, often lead to anxiety, frustration, and feelings of helplessness. Persistent infections can interfere with work, education, intimate relationships, and social participation (McLellan and Hunstad, 2016). Chronic cases are frequently associated with sleep disturbances, fatigue, and emotional distress, amplifying their personal and social burden. From a healthcare perspective, recurrent UTIs present ongoing challenges. They necessitate repeated consultations, diagnostic tests, and antibiotic prescriptions, increasing costs and resource demands (OECD, 2019). Prophylactic or long-term antibiotic regimens are sometimes used to prevent recurrence, but these strategies remain controversial. Although they may reduce short-term episode frequency, they contribute to resistance development by selecting resistant strains that perpetuate the cycle of infection (Holmes, 2016; Pigrau and Escolà-Vergé, 2020). Resistance development What happens in all these cases is that unnecessary or inappropriate courses of antibiotics increase bacterial ability to withstand future treatments, making the disease progressively harder to manage. Resistant infections often demand more costly or invasive medications, longer treatment durations, and, in severe cases, hospitalisation (ECDC and WHO, 2022). For these reasons, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is recognised as one of the most urgent global health threats. In 2019, AMR directly caused an estimated 1.27 million deaths and contributed to nearly 5 million worldwide (Murray et al., 2022). Without effective action, projections indicate that AMR could result in 10 million deaths annually by 2050, surpassing cancer as the leading cause of mortality (O’Neill, 2016). The intersection of AMR, UTIs, and antibiotic consumption becomes particularly visible when examining their combined contribution to health loss. Figure 2.2 highlights this by showing the share of deaths directly attributed to AMR related UTIs alongside the overall impact of UTIs and of AMR in 2019. The severity of the situation also needs to take into account that the burden of recurrent UTIs extends beyond individual suffering to substantial societal consequences. The communication gap in UTI diagnosis is not a minor clinical issue but a critical link between personal illness and a global health crisis. Diagnostic uncertainty and reliance on symptom-based treatment sustain empirical antibiotic use, driving the spread of resistant pathogens. Breaking this cycle requires improved ways of capturing, interpreting, and integrating patient-reported symptoms into clinical decision-making. This research addresses this challenge by examining how subjective experiences of UTIs can be more effectively represented and communicated. Focusing on the lack of awareness and communication between patients and clinicians, the thesis is interested in investigating how individual narratives influence diagnostic accuracy, treatment outcomes, and the broader dynamics of healthcare. 2.1.2 Feminist perspectives on UTIs as a gender issue UTIs affect people of all genders but disproportionately impact women, a pattern linked to anatomical, hormonal, and behavioural factors that increase both susceptibility and recurrence. However, the significance of this disparity extends beyond biology. Gender also shapes how symptoms are perceived, articulated, and evaluated within clinical practice. Women have been historically underrepresented in medical research, and their embodied accounts are often undervalued or dismissed (Almeida et al., 2020a; Perez, 2019). This contributes to a clinical culture in which women’s expressions of pain, reported more readily and with greater sensitivity than men’s, are frequently misinterpreted as emotional excess or exaggeration rather than recognised as valid indicators of illness (Bever, 2022). These biases are further perpetuated by female health technologies often embody normative assumptions about women’s bodies and reproduce structural inequalities through their design (Billuart, 2024). Taken together, these perspectives show that UTIs are not only a biomedical condition but also a gendered issue shaped by entrenched sociocultural patterns that influence how symptoms are understood, recorded, and acted upon across healthcare systems. Gender bias in data and practice Systemic gender bias permeates the production, interpretation, and application of medical data. Women have been historically excluded or underrepresented in clinical trials and large-scale datasets, resulting in treatments, technologies, and guidelines that are optimised for male physiology (Almeida et al., 2020a; Perez, 2019). This underrepresentation persists despite increasing evidence of sex-specific differences in disease presentation, drug metabolism, and treatment response, limiting the ability of healthcare systems to deliver effective and equitable care to women. Bias is not confined to data gaps but extends to the processes of data collection and use (D’Ignazio and Klein, 2020). Women are frequently positioned as passive subjects in medical research rather than as active contributors to knowledge production (Thompson, 2020). This reinforces an asymmetry between those who control data and those whose bodies generate it (Fotopoulou, 2021). Such asymmetries have tangible effects on care, as demonstrated by the fact that women are prescribed less pain medication than men following equivalent procedures, are less likely to receive appropriate diagnostic testing, and are more often dismissed or under-treated when they report symptoms (Tomasello and Almeida, 2020). The problem is not unique to UTIs, but similar patterns exist across a range of conditions. Women suffering from chronic pain are more likely to have their symptoms attributed to psychological causes rather than to physiological conditions. Evidence also indicates that women’s reported experiences of pain are often perceived as exaggerated, despite studies suggesting that women may have greater pain sensitivity and a higher propensity to report pain accurately (Samulowitz et al., 2018; Bever, 2022). These disparities reflect entrenched cultural and institutional patterns that undermine the credibility of women’s embodied experiences. Situated knowledge and feminist critique Feminist theory challenges these exclusions by highlighting the value of situated knowledge. Haraway (1988) argues that all knowledge is produced from specific, embodied standpoints, rather than from a universal, objective perspective. Within this framework, women’s experiences of UTIs are not peripheral to medical understanding but constitute essential evidence for improving diagnosis, treatment, and care. Recognising this knowledge challenges the hierarchy that places the need of quantifiable laboratory results above understanding personal experience. Historical feminist health movements laid the foundation for this critique. The Boston Women’s Health Book Collective’s 1971 publication Women and Their Bodies (later Our Bodies, Ourselves) exposed the paternalism of mainstream medicine, highlighting how opacity, authority, and mystification kept patients subordinate to medical professionals. By encouraging women to share personal experiences and reclaim knowledge about their own bodies, the collective drove a shift toward transparency, autonomy, and accountability in healthcare (Boston Women’s Health Book Collective, 1971). Contemporary feminist scholarship builds on these foundations by advocating for participatory and women-centred approaches that integrate embodied experience into research, design, and healthcare practices (Almeida et al., 2020; D’Ignazio and Klein, 2020). In design research, this shift has produced methods and tools that position women not as passive recipients but as co-creators of knowledge. The introduction of the Research and Feminism(s) track at the 2018 Design Research Society conference illustrates the growing recognition of feminist approaches as critical to rethinking how design engages with complex social issues, including health. In Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), there has been an expansion of research beyond maternal care to include topics such as menstruation, menopause, endometriosis, and chronic pelvic pain. These developments demonstrate a broader commitment to challenging exclusionary practices and to legitimising women’s embodied knowledge as a foundation for innovation. 2.1.3 Positioning this research Considering that UTIs primarily affect individuals with female anatomy due to structural and physiological factors such as shorter urethral length, hormonal fluctuations, and microbial interactions within the vaginal and urinary environments (Mohiuddin, 2019; Pigrau & Escolà-Vergé, 2020), gender becomes a critical lens through which to examine both the medical and social dimensions of this condition. While this research focuses on women’s experiences, this choice does not imply that men’s experiences of UTIs or related conditions are less significant. Rather, attending to gendered differences makes visible the need for integrated approaches that address a broader spectrum of experiences. Insights generated from women’s experiences can inform more inclusive strategies, provided that future work continues to account for the diversity of bodies, identities, and health trajectories. This perspective emphasises the importance of approaches grounded in difference, treating it as a valuable source of knowledge rather than as a deviation from a norm. For this reason, it is important to clarify that within this thesis the term “women” is used in alignment with epidemiological and biomedical literature, in which women are identified as the group most affected by UTIs. At the same time, this work recognises the limitations of binary gender categories and acknowledges that not all individuals with female anatomy identify as women, and that not all women have female anatomy. The term is therefore employed pragmatically to maintain consistency with clinical research, while explicitly respecting gender diversity. On this basis, positioning the study within feminist theory constitutes both a methodological and an ethical choice. Feminist scholarship foregrounds situated and embodied perspectives, and challenges approaches that privilege detached notions of objectivity over lived experience (Haraway, 1988; Harding, 1991). In the context of UTIs, such perspectives are particularly relevant, as diagnosis relies heavily on symptom narratives. When these narratives are marginalised, the consequences may include misdiagnosis, ineffective treatment, and recurrent infections, reinforcing existing inequities in women’s health. To respond to these challenges, the following chapter introduces methods that recognise and strengthen women’s perspectives within diagnostic and care processes, proposing design based approaches to symptoms as a way to support and enhance the value of these lived experiences.
